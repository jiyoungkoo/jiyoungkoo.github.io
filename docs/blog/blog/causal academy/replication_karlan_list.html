Replication of Karlan and List (2007)

Does Price Matter in Charitable Giving? Evidence from a Large-Scale Natural Field Experiment

A replication of key findings from Karlan and List (2007) on matching grants and charitable giving.
Author

Jiyoung Koo

Published

April 20, 2026

Introduction

Karlan and List (2007) study whether matching grants affect charitable giving. In their field experiment, prior donors to a nonprofit organization were randomly assigned to receive either a standard fundraising letter or a letter that included a matching grant offer. The main question is whether lowering the effective “price” of giving through a match increases the likelihood that people donate.

This is an important causal question because fundraisers often use matching campaigns to increase donations, but it is not obvious whether these offers truly change behavior or simply repackage the same appeal. Because the authors use random assignment, differences in outcomes between the treatment and control groups can be interpreted causally.

In this post, I replicate three parts of their analysis. First, I check whether treatment and control groups look balanced on observable variables, which helps confirm that randomization worked. Second, I replicate the main finding that matching grants increase donation rates and average revenue per solicitation. Third, I estimate a regression similar to one of the specifications in Table 3. As an additional exercise, I also visualize the treatment effect graphically and explain whether the result mainly operates through the extensive or intensive margin.

Balance Check (Replicating Part of Table 1)

A key feature of a randomized experiment is that the treatment and control groups should be similar before treatment. If that is true, then differences in outcomes after the letters are sent are more credibly caused by the matching grant itself.

I examine a few observable variables that appear in Table 1:

  • freq: number of prior donations
  • HPA: highest previous amount donated
  • dormant: indicator for whether the donor had been inactive
  • ltmedMRA: indicator for whether the donor’s recent giving was below the median
treatment Control Treatment
freq 8.047 8.035
HPA 58.960 59.597
dormant 0.523 0.524
ltmedMRA 0.488 0.497

The treatment and control groups are very similar on these observable characteristics. This supports the claim that randomization worked as intended and that there is no obvious evidence of selection bias between the two groups.

Something Additional: A Graphical Presentation of the Main Result

As an additional exercise, I present the treatment effect graphically. The paper mainly reports this result in a table, but a simple figure makes the difference easy to see.

The graph shows a clear increase in the response rate for the treatment group. The difference is small in absolute terms but meaningful relative to the low baseline donation rate.

This pattern also helps clarify the mechanism behind the main effect. The matching grant appears to operate mainly through the extensive margin: it gets more people to donate. By contrast, the conditional gift amount changes very little, so there is less evidence that the treatment strongly affects the intensive margin, or how much donors choose to give once they decide to donate.

Conclusion

This replication reproduces the main qualitative findings of Karlan and List (2007). First, the treatment and control groups appear balanced on observable characteristics, which supports the validity of the experimental design. Second, matching grants increase both the probability of donating and the average amount raised per solicitation. Third, an OLS regression confirms that the treatment effect is positive and statistically significant.

My additional graph also makes the main result easier to see and reinforces the interpretation that the effect works primarily by increasing participation rather than changing gift size among donors.

Overall, this paper provides a clear example of how randomized experiments can be used to draw causal conclusions in marketing and fundraising contexts.
